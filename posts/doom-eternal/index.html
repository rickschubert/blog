Doom Eternal - Review

Oh boy did I have a blast with Doom Eternal. Rarely have I played a game so intense that my eyes were regularly fluttering after a gaming session.

I presume there is not a lot of introduction necessary for the type of Doom Eternal’s gameplay: it is a first-person shooter which has you tear through hordes of hell demons.

The impressive thing about it is how the shooter mechanics are scaled back to the absolute essentials. There is no aim down sight feature, no weapon reloading - the game has you shooting to your heart’s desire with little restrictions. The developers understood well what makes a shooter fun and they are committed to giving you as much of this as possible.

The movement mechanics are as focused as the shooting counterpart: there is no unnecessary crouching or, god forbid, leaning around edges. Doom Eternal has you racing and strafing through the battlefields in speeds which can only be described as neck-breaking, especially when you unlock abilities which increase movement speed after melee kills or when you use the dash ability to propel from place to place like a tiny rocket ship. There is also a horizontal component to movement as you can double jump to your heart’s content. I was pleasantly reminded of multiplayer titles like the excellent Unreal Tournament.

In a way, Doom Eternal is also a strategic game: many of the enemies have specific weak points which you can target: one enemy has a protective shield which is easily destroyed with the plasma gun; another shoots rockets from a backpack which you can destroy to disable them. Thus, I found myself using almost all the weapons regularly depending on the situation. (I say almost - Ballista, you suck.) Ammunition is, especially in the beginning, relatively tight and runs out fast. You can replenish ammo by literally chainsawing through smaller demons though.

The difficulty level is a very interesting topic: I played Doom Eternal on Normal and found the challenge in the beginning to be quite hefty. My problem was that I was not yet used to the game’s mechanics and felt overwhelmed by all the capabilities the Doom Slayer possesses and which you all have to use to survive: you can melee enemies to stagger them, you have two types of grenades, there is a flame thrower which you need to spray at enemies to get shield points, you have a chainsaw, later on even a sword… the options are plenty. But once you mastered these mechanics you feel like an absolute machine. In the final sections, I was tearing myself through even the largest hordes with relative ease.

Another great thing I have to mention is how the game handles boss fights: I usually suck at boss fights in video games and also don’t enjoy them. It annoys me having to evade attacks and being forced to play by their rules instead of resorting to my own play style. Of course, this pattern is still a given in Doom Eternal, but the game offers you an increased shield in boss fights should you die too often - it does so without any judgement and the buff does not even hurt progression or rewards. While I only had to use this feature once in the penultimate boss fight, I felt incredibly relieved that they thought about this and also offered it so freely. Because let’s be honest, who enjoys repeating bosses multiple times?

Visually, the game is stunning on my Playstation 4 Pro. This is even more impressive given how fast the gameplay is and how many enemies are on screen during fights. Doom Eternal has a very distinct style which can be best compared to sleeves of 70s Heavy Metal albums. The environments are relatively distinct (there is a destroyed city, the planet Mars, an ice world, and even your own private castle which is a fricking spaceship!) and they all look pretty gloomy which delightfully contrasts with the flashy colours of the demons, explosions and collectables. It should be odd that this mix fits, but it does.

One thing which also has to be mentioned is the music which is once again composed by Mick Gordon and fits the game fantastically. It is an odd mix of heavy electronic beats and incredibly chunky guitar riffs which make me as an avid metalcore fan extremely happy.

Of course, even an excellent game has some drawbacks and in the case of Doom Eternal this is the enemy called Marauder. This enemy appears not too frequently (luckily) but is hard to kill: due to his attacks, you can neither be too far nor too close him. When he attacks you, there is a split second in which you can shoot to counter him. The annoying bit about this is that the window you have to react is very narrow, at least for me - perhaps my reaction time isn’t the fastest. Also, it takes a lot of shots to kill him. This makes the Marauder incredibly annoying. It seems like the developers knew this as well because whenever he appears, there aren’t a lot of other enemies around except for small fodder demons through which help you retrieve ammunition - of which you need a lot to defeat the Marauder.

You might have noticed that I didn’t say too much about the story - because there isn’t any. Essentially, hell demons enslave almost all of humanity and you have to stop them. I enjoyed how serious the story took itself, the game doesn’t try to crack jokes: Doom Eternal is all about trying to deliver this heavy metal fantasy that you play the most badass killing machine in the universe. Jokes and giggles would only clash with this vision.

Doom Eternal is a fantastic game and I can wholeheartedly recommend it. I bought it for £15 and I am almost inclined to get the DLC which is £15.99, more than the main game cost me - but I had that much fun with it that I think it might actually be worth it.
