Little Fires Everywhere - Book Review

It is rare that a book comes along which makes me think so much about its themes and subjects. In that way, “Little Fires Everywhere” could be on track of becoming a modern classic.

It is one of those books where the characters feel completely life-like. For a novel grounded in the reality of the 1990s in the US, the plot is quite exciting and there are plenty of revelations and events which keep you on your toe. The story discusses the tensions between two different families and their opposing ideas of what a good life means. And tensions do run high towards the end of the book, which culminates in an explosive finale. But it bothered me a bit that there are so many conflicts opened up but few of them are actually followed through to their fullest. Many conflicts brood between the lines and remain unspoken. This seems like some missed opportunity.

It is a very feminine story, which I found highly refreshing - there aren’t a lot of male characters in this book, and if so these are only side characters. The only male person with an actual personality is teenage son Moody; but we know barely anything about all other male characters; certainly not how they feel, only what they do. Even son Trip, whose role is reasonably prominent, presents himself solely as a good-looking sportsman and this is also pretty much the only reason why teenage girl Pearl desires him. Some might argue that this is only a fair reversal of the usual tropes for literature romances; others might say it’s sexism in reverse. Be that as it may.

Many reviewers suggest that “Little Fires Everywhere” talks a lot about race but I have to vehemently disagree with this. One character in this book is Asian and one minor side character is black - how would that make this book explore race? A far bigger problem is that many of these critics and also most of the characters in the book - heck, even the narrator! - seem to confuse race with culture. What, so a person of Asian race needs to know about Chinese culture when they grow up in America? Does a black Frenchman have to know about “the customs of their people”? I find it deplorable that the issue of race relations has to be projected onto every work of art even if it’s completely unrelated. In the case of “Little Fires Everywhere”, I’m sure the author enjoys this line of thought as it makes for great publicity, but I absolutely fail to see how this book discusses or even just depicts the topic of race.
