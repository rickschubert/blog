Walden (Harold Pinter Theatre) - Review

Walden brings back London theatre with a bang: First-timer Amy Berryman tells an exciting story about twin’s relationships set to the background of a dreary but believable science-fiction plot.

There are many stories popular at the moment which deal with the post-apocalyptic - Walden is different in that regard since it is a pre-apocalyptic tale. The play is about two sisters who both work (or worked) for Nasa, trying to make Mars livable for future generations due to the earth barely being inhabitable. Sister Cassie just returned from the moon while Stella used to work for Nasa but lives now with Brian, an Earth Activist who is opposing the plans of going to another planet and who believes that humanity should invest all its power into trying to save earth instead of leaving.

One of the most incredible feats about this play is that there is so much world-building involved, yet writer Amy Berryman conveys all of this while exclusively sticking to only three actors playing in a cabin in the woods. The world is smartly thought out and there are multiple global issues and political parties whose goals and beliefs we all get to learn about. This futuristic scenario feels like a punch to the stomach due to its believability.

What is even more outstanding to me is all the tension Berryman draws from only having three actors: the relationship between Cassie and Stella is complex yet believable. As a twin myself, I found their relationship never to be contrite or artificially created for the plot’s worth.

The play’s dialogue is smart, trimmed and precise, there is not a single superfluous word. I enjoyed the many cases of foreshadowing or pick-ups of earlier pieces of dialogue: as an example, Cassie is being depicted throughout as someone who tries to imitate or even steal things from her sister Stella. First, this is mentioned through means of some rocks and later on even in larger aspects of Stella’s life. Things like these stunned me in the best way possible. Scenes are fairly long but never go overboard; they always hit the right timing.

As for the actors, all three actors managed to do a great job, especially Lydia Wilson as Cassie shines. The entire cast has acted previously in movies and it shows; there is some real star quality in here. With Gemma Arterton, we even get to see a Bond girl. Impressive!

There is not a lot to complain about, the only thing I was missing was more humour. There are a few jokes in this play but they never went beyond a smirk. I also would have hoped for more romance; I feel that this could have elevated this story. I’m sure that Berryman was exactly not aiming for this, but I do believe that a tinge of sappiness would have increased its impact.

Please all go out and see this while you can, it’s a stunner!

3 June 2021
