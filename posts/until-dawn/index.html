Until Dawn - Review

I am delighted to talk about this “game” - because Until Dawn is an experience which took me by surprise, mostly due to its clever writing.

But first, let’s get out of the way what type of game Until Dawn is - or rather what type of game it is not. Until Dawn belongs into the genre of interactive movies; comparable titles are Heavy Rain, Beyond: Two Souls and Detroit: Become Human. I started the two latter games but never finished them. I grew bored with these and felt dissatisfied by how few game elements there were. The reason I gave Until Dawn nonetheless a chance was that somebody recommended and also borrowed it to me.

While other games of this genre don’t make it clear what type of experience they are, Until Dawn somewhat indicates it with its cast. I did not know going in that Rami Malek was in it, but when I fired the game up I recognised him immediately as well as Hayden Panettiere, another well-known actress. The presence of these A-list actors made it obvious that it wanted to be regarded as an interactive TV show rather than a game. The fact that Rami Malek stars in a Playstation game still baffles me, considering that he went on to star as the lead in the TV show Mr Robot the same year of release and went on to win an Academy Award a bit later.

Coming back to the “game”: Until Dawn is an interactive TV show in the style of modern gore horror movies. Eight teenagers (although none of the actors was in their teens, most male actors were even already in their 30s) meet for a weekend away in a remote lodge on top of a mountain while a winter storm is howling. Spooky and gory events are as much to be expected as are horny innuendos and shy regards.

The game’s novelty stems from its choice system, the so-called butterfly effects: Many of your choices throughout the game affect the narrative - most of them minor, but some of them actually in a major way. In fact, there is a chance that all eight teens survive the night - or none of them. Up to the middle point of the game, I was underwhelmed by the system because many of the outcomes were in the style of “You sided with character A against character B - later on, character B was angry with you.” I wouldn’t have been surprised if one outcome would have been “You chose to take out the trash - the trash stood outside in the next scene”. But this should be expected in a game of such quality, the studio can’t create hundreds of different stories; the plot still follows all the main beats no matter your choices.

The events that drastically have an effect are relating to death or survival: I was researching on YouTube after my playthrough about all the possible outcomes and I am floored by the many ways in which the story can play out. Each of your characters has multiple ways they can die and this affects subsequent scenes since entire characters are missing. Not all of these deaths relate to your choices though; many deaths can be caused by bad reactions in the game’s quick-time events. As a player, this can seem frustrating and unfair. But on the other hand, it makes for a unique experience: there is no “Game Over” screen, the story continues even if you fail. Whether you win or lose actually has meaning.

I am fascinated by the idea of making a video game with such a great cast which fully intends to closely resemble a TV show. I wonder what the financial implications of this are: Is it cheaper to create a video game than a TV show? Which can attract a wider audience?

Not all is gold and roses though with Until Dawn and I have to mention it: Technically, it is a catastrophe. The graphics are fantastic, even more so for a game from 2015, it can easily be called photorealistic. But the framerate is inexcusable; even on a Playstation 4 Pro (a system which didn’t exist yet when this game came out), the game stutters noticeably, especially in fast-paced action scenes but also during simple walking situations. Almost worse is the sound: the entire audio mix is highly uneven (jump scares are incredibly loud compared to the dialogue, even after readjusting volumes in the settings) and the dialogue sounds like it was recorded with a smartphone. I do wonder what happened here during development. If you have Academy Award-winning stars in your cast, having such a bad sound which completely muffles their performances is simply inexcusable.

I do recommend giving this interactive TV show a try, it is just £7.99 during sale events. Just don’t expect every butterfly effect to be ground-breaking or to receive grandiose gameplay elements; otherwise you will enjoy this as well!
