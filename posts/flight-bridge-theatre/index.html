Flight (Bridge Theatre) - Review

Flight at the Bridge Theatre London - Info & Tickets

Writing a negative theatre review is something I dread: Both because I want to support the arts but also because I myself am an aspiring (although yet unsuccessful) theatre writer. I know how much heart and soul goes into a play, especially into one that makes it into production. But I would also like to be honest if I don’t enjoy something. And sadly, I didn’t enjoy Flight.

The play tells the story of two Afghan boys who become refugees and try to make their way across Europe. They aim to reach London where they have an uncle who might support them. The journey is not easy though as they are being exploited by human traffickers. It is a very straightforward plot which is not told in a very interesting way when it comes to dialogue and scenes; there is barely any surprise in this tale.

The way things are presented is worth a paragraph by itself: You are being sat alone on a chair in the dark and handed some headphones. To your left and right are dividers. In front of you, you can see dioramas which revolve from right to left as the story progresses. Lighting always highlights the diorama that the story wants you to focus on. It is therefore almost as if you would be reading a comic book with dioramas for panels. Only that this comic book turns the pages on its own and is accompanied by a soundtrack.

While I enjoyed this idea tremendously, I found that this great potential has been deeply unused. Sure, there are a few dream sequences that really make use of the fact that miniatures can show things that can’t exist in reality, but apart from that most dioramas look fairly realistic. One could have played a lot more with perspective and colour, trying to show things that are not possible in other mediums. This is after all what the best comic books and animated movies revolve around - but Flight tries to be too realistic, as it does with its story.

Perhaps I should have been more cautious when booking this show but I couldn’t help myself; I desperately craved for theatre. Due to coronavirus, I only managed to see one show in an entire year. And Flight is one of the earliest theatre shows to reopen again so I thought why not book it! But in my book, I can’t even call it a theatre show. It felt more like an art installation. To me, theatre is live. I could have accepted if the audio would have been recorded with live puppeteering; but due to the diorama style of storytelling, everything in this show is recorded.

I don’t want to judge the acting in this play too much as I haven’t seen the actors, all you do is hear them. I found their performances acceptable but not outstanding. There were some sequences where I found the acting to be quite stiff, especially when characters were meant to be joking with each other.

Lastly, something I want to mention and which gravely bothered me: the volume is way too loud. In the middle of the play, I was asking an usher whether it would be possible to turn down the headphones. After a minute they came back and said it would not be an option and that I could try spreading the headphones over my ears instead. I tried that but it was annoying always having to hold these while trying to follow along. The audio was way too loud, especially when characters were screaming or hissing. The high-pitched noises hurt my ears. I find it astounding that no one would have thought about that and could have imagined that some people might not be comfortable with overly loud volumes.
