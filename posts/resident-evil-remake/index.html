Resident Evil Remake - Review

I saw this game for £3.99 in the PSN store and since it’s the festive period with a lot of time on my hands (thanks Corona), I decided to give this a try. Previously, I was playing the recent remakes of Resident Evil 2 and 3 which I deeply enjoyed.

Why do I even enjoy the franchise? It is a mystery to me as well, I’m honest. In my childhood, I have always been afraid of horror movies and games. Even Terminator, which is in hindsight not the darkest movie in the world, would have given me nightmares. The impressive part of childhood me was that I did not even try out these movies or games - just the plain knowledge of their existence made me already feel ill at ease. “Watching a movie about an alien that kills people? Thanks, I’m headed for Nopetown!”

There are two things I greatly enjoy about the Resident Evil games: the puzzles and the deeply rooted 90s flair. I’m a sucker for this decade and there is something so visceral of it in this franchise that it’s almost too hard to grasp. It’s the corny dialogue, the cliché looking monsters and zombies, the lack of always ready communication, that weird attraction for handguns, the urban environments… Resident Evil screams 90s.

The Resident Evil I played is actually a 2015 released HD remaster of the game from 2002 - which in turn was a remake of the game from 1996. Surprisingly, it doesn’t feel like it would be almost 20 years old; more like 10 years. The graphics greatly benefit from hand-drawn backgrounds. Characters look relatively dated but this just underlines the 90s flair.

The gameplay is simple: The player navigates either Chris or Jill and has to find their way out of a creepy mansion filled with zombies. (I chose Jill, I have a thing for playing as female characters.) The game uses fixed camera angles while the player explores the mansion. When pressing a button, the character stands still and points their gun at an enemy. While doing so, you are able to shoot.

During the exploration of the mansion, players encounter many locked areas which can often be accessed by means of solving some puzzle - there is a lot of backtracking involved. Exploring the mansion was by far the aspect I enjoyed the most. I loved stumbling through this creepy environment and trying to figure out how to access specific rooms and what to do with the items I found. I am proud to say that I solved almost all the puzzles on my own. In order to do so, it was important to keep notes while playing. I drew up all the maps in my notebook and noted down hints about areas. In fact, the only two puzzles I couldn’t solve were caused by incomplete notes.

While I greatly enjoyed the exploration aspect, I detested the gunplay. The player simply stands static while pointing at an enemy and can then shoot - that’s it. Depending on faster enemies, you are required to dodge attacks and evade enemies but this is much easier said than done. Initially, I started the game using the tank controls of the original in which a press to the lift stick only turns you to the left direction - like a tank! - but you don’t move into that direction. I cannot fully comprehend how the game was designed for this mechanic in the first place. Less because I would have had problems with the tank controls themselves (I didn’t, thanks Grim Fandango!) but in order to run away from enemies, you need to turn around either by 180 degrees with your stick or use the quick-turn button. But that functionality doesn’t feel fast enough to dodge enemies. Later on, there are even a few stages where the player has to run between points in a short amount of time and this would definitely not have worked with tank controls. A bit of a shame, as I actually felt that by removing those tank controls I took some form of purity away from the game.

Another negative aspect I have to mention is item management, especially save points. Throughout the game, you have only eight item slots which are way too few considering all the things you find. Therefore, it is necessary to run back and forth to your loot box to deposit items. But much worse for me is the requirement of certain items to be able to save your progression. Due to the lack of save items, I played for much longer sessions than I would have liked to - I’m an adult man, let me manage my own time! Even worse, I often couldn’t save when I wanted to as I ran out of save items. This threw me into a vicious cycle where I died multiple times a good half an hour after having last saved because I couldn’t any longer. This caused me to repeat huge chunks of the game multiple times. I despise repeating the same section in video games; this aspect was a real torture and test of patience for me.

The game involves a lot of “backtracking” - first exploring one area, finding a locked door, going to a different area, finding the key for the previously locked door and turning back. It really helps driving home the point of not being able to escape this creepy mansion but I feel like this mechanic also overstayed its welcome. In fact, this could be said for the entire game to be honest. While I enjoyed it, I also feel like it lost steam in the lost few hours.

Although there have been a lot of negative points, I still think the game is worth playing - it’s a fun (and scary!) step back in time. But be aware of what you are getting into: Resident Evil HD Remaster is not a brand new game so don’t expect the world. What you can expect is a creepy mansion, dark atmosphere, cool puzzles and cheesy acting.

I would suggest new starters to start on the easy or very easy difficulty (I played on normal) and to immediately switch to the new alternative control scheme. I say so because what I enjoyed the most about this game were the puzzles and 90s atmosphere - but these points had been turned sour for me due to the many times I had to repeat sections over and over again. An easier difficulty would have remedied that.
