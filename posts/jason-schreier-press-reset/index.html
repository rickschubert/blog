Press Reset - Review

Press Reset by video game journalist Jason Schreier details how unstable development is with its always looming layoffs, office moves across the country and project cancellations.

The book starts in the late 80s and early 90s with famous video game designer Warren Specter, detailing his move across various publishers, trying to make games he is passionate about but which never quite hit the nerve of a mass audience. From there on, the book moves forward through the decades, covering the experiences of dozens of industry figures. The interesting thing with the book’s storytelling is that you start to realise how small the industry is. Although Schreier mostly focuses on individual projects in each chapter, employee and company names repeat throughout and start to ring a bell based on earlier chapters. It becomes obvious how often employees have to jump from company to company, often across the entire American continent, uprooting their lives only for having to be laid off yet again and having to move another time.

Reading this book makes you realise how difficult it must be to create a game: Usually, multiple studios are working on the same title, often spread across different cities or even continents. Many of these games don’t even see the light of day because suddenly the publisher might decide that the project is not good enough. And if a game comes out, it is not always a given that this project is a success: not every title can be an Uncharted - or whatever your favourite game is. In fact, many of the titles mentioned in the book were unknown to me or were reported by Schreier to be a failure in some way or form. This stands completely at odds with the many sacrifices creators make as we learn throughout the book. Workers in the industry give their all, both in terms of life time and life quality, just to fulfil their dream of making games.

If you are interested too then you might start reading this article on the outlet Polygon which details the numerous ways Bioshock Infinite was a sheer catastrophe to work on, despite the overall fidelity of the final product. This article is an excerpt from Schreier’s Press Reset and I found it so fascinating that I just had to get the entire book. And I don’t regret it, I haven’t read a book which this much vigor in years. Highly recommended.
