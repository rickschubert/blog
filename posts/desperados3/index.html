Desperados III - Review

It’s about time I am reviewing Desperados III - what a game! Without this, my last week of 2020 would have looked very different. This game brought pride when I succeeded in its challenges - and anger when I failed.

Desperados III belongs to the Real-Time Strategy genre - this means that the player controls their characters from the top-down perspective and has to try to eliminate (or evade) enemies unseen. It’s essentially a stealth game from a bird’s eye view.

You can display each enemy’s view cone and have to evade these - otherwise everyone nearby will be alarmed. The essentially simple objective becomes incredibly difficult given the way enemies are positioned: they often appear in big numbers and stand in circles and other shapes so that each of your enemies has a good view onto another person. In order to succeed, you can lure people away i.e. by whistling or making them turn around to where a coin was thrown. These short moments are where you can navigate your characters to strike and eliminate the threat. That way, you chomp yourself step by step through even the biggest masses.

What makes this so difficult is that the R in RTS stands for real time - you don’t have a lot of time to react. When the moment is right, you have to strike. Yes, there is a pause mode where you can plan actions for multiple characters, but apart from certain actions, you can always just plan one task per team member. And even if you did plan meticulously, your crew still has to execute your instructions - thus it could very well be that you send them off to point A which you assumed safe in pause mode only to discover that once the action continues, the route is actually not safe anymore because an enemy turned around.

Opponents can be eliminated in various ways: you could either walk up to them and kill them from behind, you could throw a knife, shoot, have one of your characters distract them through flirting or blind them - the options are wide! Since I am so used to “pure” stealth games like Splinter Cell, it initially felt wrong to me to use guns though. Instead, I resorted to melee kills and knife throws. But this really diminished my success and I realised later on that there is only so much one can do without a bullet. It turns out that the habit of not shooting is actually hard to unlearn! During my playthrough, I often forced my way through encounters employing close-combat kills although guns would have done the job much easier and saved me a lot of pain.

Failure is encouraged by the game though, you can always quicksave and reload with the press of a button. The recap of each mission often showed me that the number of saves and loads easily went into the hundreds. And this brings me to my main gripe with the game: I love quick saving and reloading, it helps me perfecting my stealth techniques and getting an execution 100% right. But I don’t think the existence of a quick save feature should be an excuse for not testing your game - and I have the impression that the developers mostly play-tested themselves instead of observing real people. Most of the situations to defeat enemies require absolute millimetre work and cat-like timing - even doing one step just slightly wrong will already cause you to fail. This forces you to reload sections over and over although what you were doing and planning was already great - it’s just that you didn’t pick exactly the correct millisecond or click exactly the right pixel. This invites frustration.

The levels in Desperados III are extremely open and the ways you can eliminate your targets are highly variable, different players will do it differently. While this is a great thing, I suspect the developers did not consider at all how one could beat the mission when designing the levels and placing the characters - “They’ll beat it somehow, don’t worry”. To me, this is especially obvious given that the enemies' view cones are always the same size and length no matter the situation. I think the view cones should have been adjusted individually depending on the circumstances of each mission.

Enough with the complaints though, because it is all in all an excellent game. I highly enjoyed the Western atmosphere and the banter between characters. The 16 missions (each between 1 and 2 hours of playtime) all had me on their toes and I never felt bored. The game is at its best when you have all five of your characters available, but unfortunately, there is only one full mission where you get the chance to use all of your crew members for its entirety. This also makes for a lot of variety though and you get to know each of your crew’s skills and drawbacks as you can’t always resort to using the same person.

Probably one of the best things about the game: Desperados III even runs on pretty old PCs like mine. There is also a console version available but I frankly cannot imagine how this would work. I strongly required large screen resolution as well as keyboard and mouse to hand out my meticulous orders. On a console, I would probably have given up on it because I would have felt limited by the game.

Games like these which completely suck you into their mechanics are rare - I think Desperados III is a must-play. There were nights where I honestly dreamed about character positionings and days where I imagined I would see view cones on real-life passers-by. Thanks for giving me a great send-off to a bad 2020!
