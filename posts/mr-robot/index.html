Mr. Robot - Series Review

This post is divided into two sections: Section 1 contains general notes, also suitable for people who haven’t seen the show yet. Section 2 discusses the show’s major twists and should only be read by people who have seen the show.

Partition A:\General_opinion

I finally did it, I finished Mr. Robot! I don’t have a lot of focus for watching TV lately, which is why it took me slightly more than a year to finish the total of 45 episodes. And oh boy, what a ride it was!

Mr. Robot tells the story of Elliot, a brilliant hacker who tries to overthrow the mighty tech giant “E Corp” which controls the world through their products and finanical offerings. What starts as a dangerous but survivable activity turns over time into a life-threatening and crippling fight against also other organisations where it is hard to know who is friend and who is foe.

I don’t want to discuss the plot too strongly as I think it is something everyone should experience for themselves. But I can freely speak about Mr. Robot’s writing and direction. The show’s themes are always strongly divided into two: The show explores the bleak side of technological America and psychological themes relating to its characters. Over the course of the series, it feels like the tech theme takes a step back and the psychological aspects become more and more important. This was not a problem to me, but I can understand that other viewers may find it irritating, especially since the entire hacking and tech aspect has been so interesting. As a programmer myself I can attest that the hacking is usually (!) depicted quite realistically: Elliot uses real-life terminals and Linux systems, we see things like SSH keys and 2FA codes, password manglers… it is really satisfying to watch Elliot get his way with his targets. This makes one want to start learning how to hack themselves!

I enjoyed the show’s second main theme, psychology, even more. The show’s creator Sam Esmail (who nearly wrote and directed every single episode) often takes a very imaginative approach in depicting how characters feel, especially the main character: There are a lot of dream sequences, confusing montages, inter-linking dialogue and references to past events through camera angles, clothing and character positions. The dialogue is usually extremely impressive in the way that the characters don’t use too many lines, but every line is filled with meaning. Even more meaning is being evoked with the way things are directed, i.e. how characters are positioned, the speed they speak with, the music, the lighting… it is truly a directorial masterpiece. I am stunned that Esmail wrote and directed so many episodes, each of which is of the highest fidelity and quality, able to compete with major movies.

Partition B:\Major_plot_points_and_finale

Warning - This section contains spoilers of the entire show! Only read on if you already have seen it or if you are not interested in watching it.

Of course, it is the finale I would like to discuss more deeply. As a fan of how Twin Peaks Season 2 ended, Mr. Robot left me quite impressed because I felt that the show followed a similar approach: it traded the eery and uneasy feelings harboured throughout its entire run for full-blown madness and dreamscapes. I highly enjoyed this - but also can’t help feeling slapped in the face! Whenever a narrative plays with notions of reality and employs the twist that everything which was experienced previously was not real, viewers can’t help to feel cheated because they invested so much time and emotion into the story. Nobody likes it if you take that away from them!

Of course, it is never fully stated whether all the previous season’s events are imagined, but this was at least the impression I had on viewing. Let’s recap: After hacking the Deus Group, Elliot heads for the nuclear reactor to destroy Whiterose’s machine. He can simply wander into the plant without any difficulty and all he has to do is find a switched-on computer and plug in a USB stick - in this sequence, he doesn’t even type any words on the keyboard. Then he is being taken captive and has a conversation with Whiterose about society where she kills herself and starts a nuclear meltdown which Elliot can stop by solving a video game. Nevertheless, some sort of explosion happens. This explosion starts a dream sequence with a fantasy version of Elliot’s life. At the end of this dream sequence, he talks to his therapist Gloria and she reveals that Elliot is only some other split personality from the “real Elliot”, just like “Mr. Robot” was. She calls him the “Mastermind Elliot” who took care of all the hacks. Gloria pushes him to give back control to “real Elliot”. Finally, Elliot wakes up in a hospital bed with Darleen by his side.

And this is where things really go side-ways from everything we’ve seen before: it may be subtle to some, but Darleen wore a quite different set of clothing - sneakers with white soles and a somewhat fashionable overthrow. On top of that, she had two rings on her hands. This is a clothing style in which we have never seen her before, already stating that this sequence is in some form of “other world”. Elliot then asks whether everything regarding Whiterose truly happened. Darleen cries and says it was all real, Whiterose has been defeated and the Deus Group as well. But Darleen also knows that she isn’t talking to the “real Elliot” (whom we have never laid eyes on throughout the entire show) but instead to the “Mastermind Elliot” who was the designer of all the hacks in the narrative. Therefore, my theory is that Darleen was lying about these hacks being a reality just to make the “Mastermind Elliot” with all his psychological issues feel better.

The revelation that “Mastermind Elliot” is not real and just distracting the real Elliot from living his life indicates to me that everything we have seen throughout the show was not a reality. Perhaps the first few episodes in Season 1 were reality, but as soon as we always see Elliot walking around in the same set of clothes (black hoodie, black shoes, dark jeans) he becomes a distinct personality, “Mastermind Elliot”, just like Mr. Robot.

Here is where I go full-blown crazy: I believe that Tyrell Wellick’s character is the real Elliot. His entire character is very mystique and does not occur a lot throughout the show after season 1. Beginning from season 2 there is always a big secret regarding his whereabouts and he never shows up too often. But he is of extreme importance, otherwise he wouldn’t occur throughout the show. Even the final episode involves him again, where he shoots and buries the “Mastermind Elliot”. And in the sequence with the therapist Gloria, in which she tells mastermind Elliot that he is not real and just some split personality from the “real Elliot”, we even hear Tyrell Wellick distinctly say “Bonsoir, Elliot” which he told sometime in season 2 on the telephone. And season 2 was also the season where we started to realise how unreliable Mastermind Elliot is as a narrator - may I just remind the viewer of Elliot’s stay at his mothers and his engagement with the leader of a darknet website which turned out to be a fantasy and a warped understanding of the fact that Elliot was actually imprisoned. My belief is that when Darleen finally sees the real Elliot in the very last scene of the show that she sees Tyrell Wellick’s character. In fact, I was waiting throughout this entire scene that we would soon see Martin Wallström (Tyrell’s actor) replace Elliot in the back-and-forth cuts between him and Darleen. I also think that in the closeup of Darleen’s and Elliot’s hand during this sequence that the arm we see is not the one of Rami Malek (Mastermind Elliot’s actor) but instead of Martin Wallström.

But this is what I enjoy so much about this show’s finale: It gives you food for thought and allows you to come up with your own understanding of its events. I would be excited to hear from others what they have to say - what is your conclusion?
